# Phase 16: Job Preparation

## Part 1: Portfolio Review — The "Final Polish"

**Concept:** Your portfolio is your digital storefront. It must be immaculate.

**The Portfolio Audit Checklist:**

**1. GitHub Repository:**

*  All README files are complete, well-written, and error-free.

*  Code is clean, commented, and organized in folders.

*  Jupyter Notebooks have outputs cleared (`Kernel -> Restart & Clear Output`).

*  A `requirements.txt` or `environment.yml` is included.

*  A `.gitignore` file is present (to exclude large data files and secrets).


**2. Live Dashboards (Power BI / Tableau Public):**
*  All dashboards are accessible via a public link.

*  Dashboards are responsive and load quickly.

*  Business-friendly language is used (no technical jargon in titles).

*  A "Last Updated" timestamp is visible.


**3. Case Studies / Portfolio Website:**
*  You have at least one detailed case study (5+ pages).

*  Your portfolio website (if you have one) is clean, professional, and links to all your projects.


**Common Mistakes:**

*   **Broken links.** (Check every single link before sending applications).

*   **Unprofessional usernames.** (Use your real name or a professional alias).

*   **Outdated projects.** (Remove old, low-quality projects; keep only your best 3).


**Action Step:** Spend 1 full day auditing and polishing your portfolio. Ask a friend to review it and test all links.

## Part 2: Resume & LinkedIn Review — The "ATS" Pro 

**Concept:** Your resume and LinkedIn must be optimized for both humans and machines (ATS).

**Resume Checklist:**

* **Format:** Single-column, clean, .docx or .pdf. (Avoid tables and images).

* **Keywords:** Copied from the job descriptions you are targeting.

* **Quantified Achievements:** Every bullet point has a number. (e.g., "Increased sales by 15%").

* **Summary:** A compelling 3-sentence "hook" at the top.

* **Skills Section:** Packed with keywords (SQL, Python, Power BI, etc.).

* **Projects Section:** At least 2 detailed projects with links to GitHub/dashboards.

* **Proofread:** Zero typos. (Get a second pair of eyes).


**LinkedIn Checklist:**

* **Professional Headshot:** High-quality, friendly, professional.

* **Headline:** "Data Analyst | SQL, Python, Power BI | Driving Business Decisions with Data."

* **About Section:** 3-4 paragraphs telling your story.

* **Featured Section:** Pin your top 3 projects.

* **Experience:** Mirrors your resume, but slightly more conversational.

* **Open to Work:** Activate the "Open to Work" green banner.

* **Recommendations:** Request 2-3 recommendations from past colleagues or managers.

* **Activity:** Like, comment, and post at least once a week.


**Action Step:** Run your resume through Jobscan (or a similar ATS scanner) against your target job description. Aim for an 80%+ match rate.

## Part 3: Mock Interviews — The "Pressure Test"

**Concept:** Interviews are a performance. You must practice under pressure.

**Mock Interview Protocol:**

**1. Technical Mock (SQL/Python/Pandas):**

*   Use platforms like **LeetCode**, **HackerRank**, or **DataLemur**.

*   Set a timer (30 minutes) and solve 3 problems.

*   Record yourself solving a problem aloud. (Watch the recording to catch verbal tics).


**2. Behavioral Mock (STAR Stories):**

*   Create a document with 5-7 STAR stories that cover:

    *   A time you solved a complex problem.

    *   A time you made a mistake and learned from it.

    *   A time you worked with a difficult stakeholder.

    *   A time you led a project.

    *   A time you used data to influence a decision.

*   Practice answering these questions out loud (record yourself).

*   Have a friend or mentor conduct a mock interview with you.


**3. Case Study Mock (Business Problem):**

*   Download a sample dataset and give yourself 2 hours to explore it.

*   Present your findings to a friend in 10 minutes (as if they are the CEO).


**Common Mistakes:**

*   **Rambling.** (Use the STAR method to stay concise).

*   **Forgetting the "R" (Result) in STAR.** (Always quantify the outcome).

*   **Not asking clarifying questions.** (It shows engagement).


**Action Step:** Schedule a mock interview with a friend, mentor, or a service like **Pramp** or **Interviewing.io** this week.

## Part 4: Coding Assessments & Take-Home Assignments — The "Survival Guide"

**Concept:** Many companies require a technical assessment before the interview.

**The Strategy:**

**1. Coding Assessments (LeetCode/HackerRank):**

*   **Focus on SQL:** 80% of Data Analyst assessments are SQL-based. Practice Window Functions, CTEs, and complex joins.

*   **Python:** Practice Pandas manipulations (groupby, merge, pivot, apply) and basic data cleaning.

*   **Time Management:** If you get stuck on a problem, move on and come back. Partial credit is better than zero.


**2. Take-Home Assignments (The "Weekend" Project):**

*   *The Golden Rule: A take-home assignment should not take more than 4 hours. If it seems like it will, clarify the scope with the recruiter.*

*   **Step 1:** Read the entire prompt twice. Clarify any ambiguities.

*   **Step 2:** Spend 1 hour planning your approach.

*   **Step 3:** Spend 2 hours coding/analyzing.

*   **Step 4:** Spend 1 hour preparing the deliverable (presentation slides or a Jupyter Notebook with clear commentary).

*   **The Edge:** Always include a "Next Steps" section at the end. (e.g., "If I had more time, I would build a dashboard and run a logistic regression model.").


**Common Mistakes:**

*   **Over-engineering the solution.** (They want to see your thinking, not your perfectionism).

*   **Not documenting your process.** (Use comments and markdown cells).

*   **Missing the deadline.** (Submit early if possible).

## Part 5: Salary Negotiation — The "Money Talk"

**Concept: You are a professional. You are selling a valuable product (your time and skills). Negotiation is not aggressive; it is *professional*.**

**The Salary Negotiation Framework:**

**1. Do Your Research:**

*   Use **Glassdoor**, **Levels.fyi**, and **LinkedIn Salary** to understand the market rate for your role, experience, and location.

*   Know your "Walk-Away" number (the minimum you would accept).


**2. Never Give the First Number:**

*   **Recruiter:** "What are your salary expectations?"

*   **You:** "I'm open to discussing compensation. Based on my research and the value I can bring to the team, I would expect a competitive offer in line with the market. What is the budget range for this role?"

**3. The "Anchor" Tactic:**

*   If they force you to give a number, give a range. (e.g., "I'm looking for a base salary in the range of $85,000 to $95,000, depending on the total compensation package.").

**4. Negotiate the Whole Package:**

*   Don't just focus on base salary. Consider:

    *   Bonus structure.

    *   Equity / Stock options.

    *   Signing bonus.

    *   Remote work flexibility.

    *   Professional development budget.

    *   Vacation time.


**5. The "Counter-Offer" Script:**
*   *Recruiter:* "We can offer you $85,000."

*   *You:* "Thank you for the offer. I am very excited about the role and the company. Based on my research and my experience with [mention a specific skill], I was hoping for a base salary of $92,000. Is there flexibility in the budget to bridge this gap?"


**Common Mistakes:**

*   Accepting the first offer.

*   Not negotiating non-salary benefits.

*   Getting emotional.


**Action Step:** Practice your negotiation script out loud. Research the market rates for your target roles.

## Part 7: Career Growth Roadmap — The "5-Year Plan"

**Concept:** Getting the job is the beginning, not the end. You must continue to grow.

**The Career Growth Framework:**

*   **Year 1-2 (The "Foundational" Phase):**

    *   Focus on mastering your current role.

    *   Become the "go-to" person for SQL and dashboarding.

    *   Build relationships with stakeholders.

*   **Year 3-4 (The "Specialization" Phase):**

    *   Choose a specialization: Marketing Analytics, Product Analytics, Supply Chain, etc.

    *   Start mentoring junior analysts.

    *   Learn advanced topics (Big Data, Cloud, Machine Learning).

*   **Year 5+ (The "Leadership" Phase):**

    *   Transition to Senior Data Analyst, Lead, or Manager.

    *   Focus on strategy, not just execution.

    *   Build a data culture within your organization.


**Continuous Learning Plan:**

*   **Weekly:** Read 1 data-related article (Medium, Towards Data Science).

*   **Monthly:** Complete 1 coding challenge (LeetCode/HackerRank).

*   **Quarterly:** Complete 1 course or certification (Coursera, DataCamp, Microsoft PL-300).

*   **Annually:** Build 1 new portfolio project.

*   **Networking:** Attend 1 data meetup or conference per year (virtual or in-person).

## Interview Questions (Job Search Specific)

**1. Question: "What are you looking for in your next role?"**

*   *Expected Answer:* Be specific. "I am looking for a role where I can use my SQL, Python, and Power BI skills to drive business decisions. I am passionate about [Industry/Function] and would love to work for a company that values data-driven decision-making."


**2. Question: "Why do you want to work here?"**

*   *Expected Answer:* Show you've done your research. "I have been following your company's growth in [Industry/Region]. I am particularly impressed by your [Product/Service] and how you are using data to [Achievement]. I believe my skills in [Skills] can contribute to that mission."


**3. Question: "What are your salary expectations?"**

*   *Expected Answer:* Use the anchoring technique. "Based on my research and the value I can bring, I am looking for a base salary in the range of $85,000 to $95,000, depending on the total compensation package."


**4. Question: "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"**

*   *Expected Answer:* Show ambition and a growth mindset. "In 5 years, I see myself as a Senior Data Analyst or Analytics Manager, leading a team of analysts and helping the company make data-driven strategic decisions. I want to continue developing my skills in predictive analytics and storytelling."
